## Synopsis

It's move-in day at Ridgeline Academy, and player has exactly one day to figure out who they're going to be here. Between a hallway collision with the school's magnetic golden-child Jordan, a lunchroom seating decision, a group project pairing with awkward outsider Ben, and a public moment that tests everyone's silence, player's small choices quietly build toward one of three very different versions of 'fitting in.'

**Themes:** Belonging, Authenticity, First Impressions

## Cast

- **Riley Nakamura-Torres** (`player`, 12, she/they (player can select he/she/they at game start; default profile uses she/they)) — Player-controlled protagonist whose choices in seating, partnering, and moments of silence/speech determine which version of 'fitting in' they build toward — the performer, the quiet observer, or the genuine connector. Riley is the emotional lens through which the player experiences Ridgeline Academy.
- **Jordan Cho-Williams** (`Jordan`, 13, he/him) — Jordan is the gravitational center of Ridgeline's social world — the character whose approval feels like the fastest, easiest path to belonging. Functionally, Jordan is the personification of the 'performer' path: charismatic, low-cost validation that always has a hidden toll (Ben, Maya, and eventually the player's own sense of self). Jordan is not a villain — he's a mirror for the player's own temptation to trade authenticity for ease.
- **Maya Oyelaran** (`Maya`, 12, she/her) — Maya is the emotional anchor of the 'genuine connector' path — she represents the risk and reward of choosing quiet authenticity over easy, loud belonging. She never chases the player's attention the way Jordan's orbit does; she has to be noticed and chosen, which makes her arc a direct test of whether the player is capable of the smaller, harder kind of courage.
- **Elena Alvarez** (`Ms. Alvarez`, 34, she/her) — Ms. Alvarez is the well-meaning adult authority whose imperfect attention creates the exact vacuum where the player's choice matters most. She's not oblivious by design — she's a real teacher managing a real room, and her missed moment with Ben isn't negligence, it's the ordinary failure of an adult who can't watch everything at once. Her approving nod at the end of 'Out Loud' matters because it's rare and earned, not automatic — she notices real courage when she catches it, even if she doesn't catch everything.
- **Ben Korlewski** (`Ben`, 12, he/him) — Ben is the moral pressure-test of the episode — not a prize to be won or a lesson to be learned, but a real kid whose reactions (relief, retreat, quiet trust) give the player's choices weight. He is the visible cost of the 'performer' path and the visible reward of the 'connector' path.
