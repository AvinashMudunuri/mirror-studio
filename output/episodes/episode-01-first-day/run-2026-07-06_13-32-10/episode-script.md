## Synopsis

It's your first day at Ridgeview High, and every hallway, lunch table, and group project is a tiny referendum on who you're going to be here. When the school's quiet, overlooked artist Reyna becomes an easy target for the popular crowd, you'll have to decide whether fitting in is worth the cost of showing up for someone else — and for yourself.

**Themes:** Belonging, Authenticity, First Impressions

## Cast

- **Jordan Diallo** (`player`, 13, she/her) — Player-character protagonist of 'First Bell.' Jordan is the audience surrogate navigating the social ecosystem of a new school in real time. Her defining choice — whether to stay quiet and blend in with the popular crowd, or step in when Reyna becomes a target — is the emotional spine of the episode, forcing the player to weigh belonging against integrity on day one.
- **Marcus Webb** (`marcus`, 14, he/him) — Marcus is the gravitational center of the episode's 'easy belonging' path - the social gatekeeper whose approval is the tempting, cheap shortcut to feeling like you've already won on day one. He's not a villain; he's a kid performing a role he half-inherited and is quietly scared of losing, which makes his cruelty toward Reyna reflexive and social rather than personal - and makes him capable, in small moments, of being caught off guard by someone with the nerve to just... not play along.
- **Reyna Castillo** (`reyna`, 13, she/her) — Reyna is the emotional stakes of 'First Bell' made human — the quiet test of whether Jordan will choose easy belonging or real integrity. She's not a symbol of victimhood; she's a specific, funny, guarded kid who has learned to expect very little from new people and is quietly, carefully hopeful that Jordan might be different.
- **Devon Park** (`devon`, 13, he/him) — Devon is the low-stakes, unexpected friendship of the episode — proof that not every relationship at a new school has to be a performance or a test. He gives Jordan (and the player) a version of belonging that doesn't cost anything, which makes the contrast with Marcus's crowd sharper: Devon never asks Jordan to be anyone but herself, and that ease is quietly radical. He also models a version of self-protection through invisibility that mirrors what Reyna does more visibly — giving the player a second, gentler lens on the same fear.
- **Ms. Sofia Alvarez** (`ms-alvarez`, 29, she/her) — The well-meaning adult authority whose distraction — not malice — creates the vacuum where the episode's central choice has to happen. Her lateness in noticing Reyna's public humiliation isn't a plot contrivance; it's who she is under real classroom conditions, and it puts the moral weight of the moment squarely on Jordan rather than letting an adult solve it.
