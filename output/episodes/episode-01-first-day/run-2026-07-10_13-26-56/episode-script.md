## Synopsis

On the first day at Crestwood High, a transfer student must decide who to sit with, who to stand up for, and who to become before anyone else decides it for them. A single lunch period sets off a chain of choices that determines whether the player finds belonging through popularity, authenticity, or something harder and more honest in between.

**Themes:** Belonging, Authenticity, First Impressions

## Cast

- **Wren Okafor-Silva** (`player`, 13, she/her) — Player-character protagonist of 'First Bell.' Her practiced first-day survival system collides with real choices — who to sit with, who to defend, who to become — forcing her to notice, maybe for the first time, that the performance has a cost.
- **Jordan Oyelaran** (`jordan`, 13, he/him) — The warm first contact who represents 'belonging' as ease and popularity-by-proximity — a low-stakes ally whose limits (avoiding real conflict) surface right when the player needs someone to hold ground, making room for the player's own growth to matter more.
- **Alex Rivera-Chen** (`alex`, 13, they/them) — Alex is the emotional stakes-bearer of 'First Bell' — the human cost that makes the player's social choices matter. Alex is not a lesson in tolerance; they're a specific, guarded kid testing whether this new place will go the way the last one did. Every branch of the episode is really a referendum on whether Wren notices Alex, and what she does with that noticing — from a passing glance in the hallway to a real, if fragile, friendship forged under pressure in the library.
- **Maya Delacroix-Thompson** (`maya`, 13, she/her) — Maya is the gravitational center of the popular table and the primary test of the player's spine — not a villain, but a mirror of what Wren's own survival instincts could calcify into if she never risks anything. Every scene with Maya is a referendum on whether the player mistakes magnetism for belonging.
- **David Alvarez** (`mr-alvarez`, 34, he/him) — The well-meaning adult architect of the episode's central pressure points — he creates both the vulnerable spotlight moment and the forced-collaboration reckoning, not out of cluelessness but out of a genuine, slightly clumsy belief that discomfort now prevents isolation later. He's not neutral scenery; his choices actively shape the social chessboard the player has to navigate, and his own history with being the outsider quietly parallels Wren's arc without ever being stated outright.
