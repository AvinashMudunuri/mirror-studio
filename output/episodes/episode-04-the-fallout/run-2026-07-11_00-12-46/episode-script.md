## Synopsis

A joke that costs more than Wren realizes in the moment kicks off a week of forced proximity, awkward repair attempts, and one very deliberate seating chart courtesy of Mr. Alvarez. When Wren is paired with Alex — the exact person she just publicly embarrassed — she has to figure out whether an apology is a thing you say once or a thing you keep proving, especially when the easiest laugh in the room is still sitting right there, waiting to be taken again.

**Themes:** Conflict Repair, Apology, Boundaries

- **Maya Delacroix-Thompson** (`maya`, 13, she/her) — Maya is the gravitational center of the popular table and the primary test of the player's spine — not a villain, but a mirror of what Wren's own survival instincts could calcify into if she never risks anything. - **Jordan Oyelaran** (`jordan`, 13, he/him) — The warm first contact who represents 'belonging' as ease and popularity-by-proximity — a low-stakes ally whose limits (avoiding real conflict) surface right when the player needs someone to hold ground, making room for the player's own growth to matter more.
- **Alex Rivera-Chen** (`alex`, 13, they/them) — Alex is the emotional stakes-bearer of 'First Bell' — the human cost that makes the player's social choices matter. Alex is not a lesson in tolerance; they're a specific, guarded kid testing whether this new place will go the way the last one did. Every branch of the episode is really a referendum on whether Wren notices Alex, and what she does with that noticing — from a passing glance in the hallway to a real, if fragile, friendship forged under pressure in the library.
- **David Alvarez** (`mr-alvarez`, 34, he/him) — The well-meaning adult architect of the episode's central pressure points — he creates both the vulnerable spotlight moment and the forced-collaboration reckoning, not out of cluelessness but out of a genuine, slightly clumsy belief that discomfort now prevents isolation later. He's not neutral scenery; his choices actively shape the social chessboard the player has to navigate, and his own history with being the outsider quietly parallels Wren's arc without ever being stated outright.
